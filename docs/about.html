On building systems that remember

I keep returning to one question: how do we capture knowledge and make it useful to people? That question has guided a lifetime of work — from quick experiments on early home computers to systems that must survive organizational change and personnel turnover.

The lesson of generations

Different eras taught different constraints. Early programming rewarded immediacy: fast feedback let ideas iterate. Enterprise systems taught durability and the costs of brittleness: integrations, messaging, and operations require discipline. Architecture taught me to view systems as conversations — between teams, services, and users. AI now shows me the limits of generation without grounding: outputs must connect back to evidence to be trusted.

Principles I rely on

Over time I’ve distilled a few practical principles that recur in my work:

  • Capture context: save the signals and traces that explain why a choice was made.
  • Design for retrieval: memory is only valuable if it can be found and verified.
  • Layer governance: make policy and validation part of runtime, not an afterthought.
  • Prefer modularity: build components that can be swapped and evolved independently.

Projects as focused questions

Each project is an experiment that asks the central question in a specific domain. OIP treats memory as infrastructure; Delivery Wizard treats delivery artifacts as records that inform future work; Chronicle explores how personal narratives can be rooted in source material; Athena is about retention and feedback; EventEase focuses on applying these ideas in dependable business systems.

Organizations forget faster than they learn. My work aims to slow that forgetting.
